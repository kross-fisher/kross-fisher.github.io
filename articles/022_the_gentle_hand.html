The Gentle Hand

by Timothy S. Arthur

Once upon a time, as I was passing through a district of country where not many people lived, night came before I knew it. Being on foot, I could not hope to get to the village toward which I was going until a late hour. I therefore preferred seeking shelter for a night in the first house that I came to.

It had grown dark, when I found myself near a house. From the small uncurtained windows of the house the light shone. I imagined how cheerfuland comfortable it would be in that house. The house stood within a yard, and a short distance from the road along which I was moving with tired feet.

I turned aside and went to the yard. The gate was not locked. I passed through it and aproached the house. The door closed slowly by itself. When I had nearly reached the porch in front of the house, I saw a slim girl, who had noticed my entrance, stood waiting for my arrival.

As soon as the gate shut, I heard a deep, quick bark, and, suddenly, the form of a big dog appeared in the doorway. At the instant when he was about to jump, a light hand was laid upon his hairy neck, and a low word spoken.

"Go in, Tiger." said the girl, not in a voice of order, yet from her tones I could tell that she knew she would be obeyed. As she spoke, she gently touched the animal with her hand, and he turned away and disappeared within the house.

"Who's that?" A rough voice asked the question; and now a heavy-looking man took the dog's place in the door.

"How far is it to G--?" I asked, not daring to say, in the beginning, that I sought a resting-place for the night.

"To G--!" said the man, but not so cruelly as at first. "It's good six miles from here."

"A long distance; and I'm a stranger, and on foot," said I. "If you can make room for me until morning, I will be very thankful."

I saw the girl's hand move quickly up his arm, until it rested on shoulder, and now she leaned to him still closer.

"Come in. We'll try what can be done for you." There was a change in the man's voice that made me wonder. I entered a large room, in which there was a warm fire. Before the fire sat two children, who turned to look at me, with not very welcome greeting. A middle-aged woman was standing at a table, and two children were amusing themselves with a cat on the floor.

"A stranger, mother," said the man who had given me so rude a greeting at the door; "And he wants us to let him stay all night."

The woman looked at me doubtfully for a few moments, and then replied coldly, "We don't keep a hotel."

"I'm aware of that, madam," said I, "But it is night, and it's a long way yet to G--."

"Too far for a tired man to go on foot," said the master of the house, kindly, "So it's no use talking about it, mother; we must give him a bed."

I noticed the girl came to her mother's side. What she said to her I did not hear, for she spoke in a low voice; but I noticed, as she spoke, one small, fair hand rested on the woman's hand.

Was there magic in that touch? The woman's cold face changed into one of kindly welcome, and she said: "Yes, it's a long way to G--. I guess we can find a place for him."

Many times more during that evening, I observed the magic power of that hand and voice -- the one gentle yet powerful as the other. On the next morning, breakfast being over, I was preparing to leave when my host told me that if I would wait for half an hour he would give me a ride in his wagon to G--, as he had to go there on business. I was very pleased to accept the invitation.

At last the farmer's wagon was driven into the road before the house, and I was invited to get in. I noticed the horse as a rough-looking Canadian pony. He looked rather stubborn. As the farmer took his seat by my side, the family came to the door to see us off.

"Dick!" said the farmer in a hard voice, giving the rein a quick pull as he spoke. But Dick did not move a step. "Dick! Get up." And the farmer's whip cracked sharply by the pony's ear.

This did not get the pony moving, however. Dick stood firmly, without obeying. Next he impatiently brought the whip down upon the pony; but the pony only moved up a little. The man beat the pony several times. He might as well have beaten the wagon, for all his efforts had been made.

A child now came out into the road, and, catching Dick by rein, pulled him hard forward, but Dick still would not obey. He planted his fore feet more firmly and at a sharper angle with the ground.

The impatient boy now struck the pony on the side of the head with his hand, and pulled cruelly at its rein. It was no use, however; Dick just would not move.

"Don't do so, John!" I turned my head as the girl's sweet voice reached my ear. She was passing through the gate into the road, and, in the next moment, had taken hold of the child and drawn him away from the animal. She did not use strength in this; she took hold of his arm, and he obeyed her wish willingly.

And now that soft hand was laid gently on the pony's neck, and a single low word spoken. How immediately were the tense muscle relaxed -- how quickly the stubborn air disappeared.

"Poor Dick!" said the girl, as she stroked his neck lightly; or softly patted it with a childlike hand. "Now, go along, you annoying fellow!" she added, in a half-blaming, yet affectionate voice, as she drew up the rein.

The pony turned toward her, and rubbed his head against her arm for an instant or two; then, sticking up his ears, he started off cheerfully, and went on his way as freely as if nothing unpleasant had happened.

"What a wonderful power that hand has!" said I, speaking to my companion, as we rode away.

He looked at me for a moment, as if he was surprised by my remark. Then a light came into his face, and he said briefly, "She's good! Everybody and everything loves her."

Was that, indeed, the secret of her power?

Was the quality of her soul felt in her hand, even by beasts? The farmer's explanation was certainly the true one. Yet have I ever since wondered, and still do wonder, at the strength which lay in that girl's magic touch. I have seen something of the same power, showing itself in the loving and the good, but never so strong as shown in her, whom, for want of a better name, I must still call "Gentle Hand".
