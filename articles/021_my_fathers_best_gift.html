My Father's Best Gift

by Nellie Pike Randall

He had given me the document about six months ago. Now he wanted it and I wasn't sure I wanted to give it to him. I stood in the hospital hall, unfolded the paper and started reading:

To my family, doctor, and lawyer; to any individual who may become responsible for my health and welfare: Realizing that death is a certain thing and as much a part of life as being born, if the time comes when I can no longer take part in decisions for my own future, I wish this statement to stand as an expression of my wishes while still of clear mind...

I couldn't go on. I folded the paper and walked to the window. I looked out, but didn't see the trees and the flowers and the parking lot below. All I could see was his face, and remember.

Tuesdays and Fridays he went to senior-citizens meetings. Wednesdays he lunched and played cards at the Retirees Club. Weekends he accompanied various widows around town. Mondays and Thurdays he spent with me. My mother died after 63 years of marriage. He was independent, smart and fun to be with.

He carried my groceries to the car, and paid for our lunches at McDonald's. I cut his hair, cleaned his house, ate the potatoes that he cooked "in their jackets" for me.

One day, he brought me a piece of paper from the lawyer at the Senior-Citizens Center. I didn't like it, but he made me take it and then said, "Promise to bring it to me when I tell you."

He was a special reason and I loved him very much. He was my dad.

Although he was smart in almost everything else, Dad ignored obvious symptoms that he was not well. It became a struggle for him to get his swollen feet into his shoes. Frequent pains struck him in the stomach. His appetite shrank. "Nothing tastes good." he explained. After being away from home for several hours he would be out of the car and into the bathroom before I could even get to the house. I worried and told him to be careful. He ignored me. But one day the pain became greater than his will to ignore it. He growned as I called the doctor.

After the test results came in, we met with the doctor, who explained that Dad had cancer: "It's probably spread to other parts of the body."

Dad took hold of my hand as he calmly asked, "How long?"

"It's hard to say." The doctor was as pratical as Dad. "Sometimes operation and medicine can slow the growth. But the most that I can promise is to make you more comfortable. I would like you in the hospital as soon as possible."

A week later, more tests prove that the doctor was correct. Two weeks and three operations later Dad came home from the hospital, more comfortable but with no promises. My sister and I took turn staying nights and cooking for him until he said, "Enough. Go home. I'm fine." It was the first time he had ever lied to me. But I understood. He wanted the remaining time to be as normal as possible. He wanted to be able to get up in the night, walk the floor and drink a can of beer without someone there saying, "You really shoudn't do that, you know."

He took naps. He worked word games and played cards at the kitchen table. Whatever his thoughts were, he bore them alone.

We went for rides. He tired easily and waited in the car while I shopped for groceries. At McDonald's he talked to babies and talked with their grandmothers. One day he let me carry our plates, and I knew he was getting weaker. He had been home for 37 days.

That evening I went to his house to check on him. He was sitting at the kitchen table, not reading or playing cards, just sitting. I asked if he was all right. His voice shook as he answered, "No."

I hadn't thought he would admit it. It scared me, and I knew why I had come. Dad was violently sick until 4 a. m., when he finally agreed to go to the hospital.

This time he was in for seven weeks. He had three more operations. Each day he would hold my hand and talk. One day he said, "Dear, yesterday was your birthday and I didn't get you a present."

No present? Every day of my life had been a gift from him. His lap had been my refuge from lightning and thunder. His arms had sheltered me from teen-age heartbreak. His wisdom and understanding had supported me as an adult.

No present, Dad? You gave me life and helped me to live it. But I couldn't tell him. My throat ached, and my eyes burned. He closed his eyes before the tears fell out of mine.

The doctor suggested one more operation to make him live a little longer.

I was sitting beside Dad's bed, listening. When we were along, Dad spoke: "I've waited almost too long. Remember that paper I gave you?" He didn't ask what I thought about it. He simply said, "Bring it to me."

In the hall outside his room I finished reading the document:

"I fear hopeless pain more than death itself. I therefore ask that proper medicine should be used to help relieve my suffering. And if there is no hope that I will recover, I request that I be allowed to die with dignity and not be alive by artificial means."

I tried to put myself in his place, knowing what he knew. It was hard, but I finally admitted that it was his right to sign the paper. His life. His decision.

He sat on the bed, his thin legs with his swollen feet, cold and blue, hanging over the edge. Several nurses stood quietly by as he signed his name and had the signature witnessed. The nurses did not express agreement or disagreement, just acceptance.

From that day, they became afraid to come to Dad's room. They wandered in, touched him, relayed hospital news or asked him if he wanted anything. He allowed just nurse to help him, his niece, who came twice a day. She would call him pet names. His doctor continued to visit him daily, but more as a friend than a doctor. He had accepted Dad's decision.

It was different with the cancer doctor. As a cancer specialist he could not give up. He begged Dad to change his mind, speaking of the rapid advancement in cancer treatment. Dad should have the operation. Perhaps something, maybe tomorrow, would be discovered.

Dad listened. He was weak but firm, and well aware of what he was doing. He seemed to relax, as if a burden had been lifted, and he entered a period of waiting.

Late one afternoon, three weeks after he had signed the paper, I kissed him as I left, and he smiled and said, "I'll see you later."

When I returned that evening I saw that he was still and silent. He had turned his face to the window. The sun had just set, and the sky was a beautiful color. Just for him, I thought, to celebrate his freedom.

I closed my eyes as the nurse said, "He is gone."

He had left me a precious heritage: a sense of humor, a love for life, and the courage to live it. But most of all he filled in me a sense of honor that keeps me faithful to my responsibilities.

One of those responsibilities had been to allow him to make his own decision. He wanted to be remembered alive, aware, smiling at me, not as just a body connected to life-support systems.

His decision was the last and best gift he gave to me. All my memories are good. He lived the way he wanted and died the way he chose.
