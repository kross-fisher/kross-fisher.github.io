My Father's Way

by Gary Allen Sledge

Pooch was my father's dog, a big, happy dog. She wore spots of black and brown on her coat of short, white hair, and was tall enough to meet me nose. My father loved Pooch for some unlikely promise he saw in her. I saw only trouble.

Pooch was clumsy as an ox and quite affectionate. I had to try every means to avoid her kisses. She was ten poinds heavier than I was, and sometimes she knocked me down. As an eight-year-old, I found such behavior unsuitable.

That summer it was my job to take care of Pooch, since Dad was away during the week at "the Mountain", his sawmill on 200 acres of redwood and pine above the Russian River on the northern California coast. Mom and I stayed home because she didn't want me "growing up lonely and wild in the forest".

Dad wore an old, brown hat, and with one side over his eye, he had the sharp, don't-talk-back-to-me look. I was very much afraid of him.

"You mind your mother." he'd warn me before leaving for the Mountain every Monday morning.

"Yes, sir."

"You water the lawn, hear, every day. Cut it on Wednesday."

"Yes, sir."

"And Pooch needs some of the dog biscuits for her teeth and a walk morning and night." A walk with Pooch was like being dragged by a steamroller out of control.

"You're the man of the house, understand?" I said I did, but I didn't quite see how spending valuable summer hours watering the lawn and feeding his dog meant I was the man of the house.

When Dad left, Pooch always danced around his legs, with his tail beating like a thick rope against a flagpole in her eager, sad good-bye. Dad would kneel to receive her kisses while he stroked her ears. I thought it was disgusting to be kissed by a dog.

Dad had groun up quick and tough, making his own way in the world, never asking a penny from anyone. He worked his way from Oklahoma to California, met and married my mother when he was 18 and then was called up to war.

I was born a few months after he sailed out to Saipan, and I grew accustomed to be spoiled by a circle of females -- mother, grandmother and a half-dozen aunts. My father's return when I was almost three interrupted that.

In some ways, we failed to live up to one another's expectations. He wanted a rough son who could fish and hunt, while I was a bookworm who wanted a father to hold me in his lap and read to me. Taking care of Pooch was his attempt to make me tough and teach me responsibility. I hated the task.

Dad said that some day when he had time, he was going to train her. But the one time he took Pooch duck hunting, she came back with her tail drooping and a look of terrible shame on her sad-eyed face. It seemed that at the gunshot Pooch had sat down and begun to shake. Still, Dad did not give up on her. "That dog has excellent intelligence," he was fond of saying. "All I've got to do is teach her self-control."

Pooch lived in a big doghouse that Dad had built behind a fence in our back yard. I hated going into those dirty grounds to feed that dog. Every morning I'd try to steal in and get her water dish while she was sleeping. Only she was never sleeping. Out she'd jump, waving her tail, putting her foot in the dish and her paws on my chest.

Always she'd jump for the gate before I could close it, for Pooch loved freedom more than anything. Then I had to run after her for at least ten minutes around the yard.

Sometimes Pooch would jump over our five-foot-tall wooden fence, turn round and come jumping back. She'd keep that up -- with her tongue dropping and tail sticking up straight -- until she was out-of-breath. It was hard to have much respect for that dog.

When Dad was home, he looked tired and anxious. I had some idea that money was the problem, and the mill was not making much of it. Late Sunday nights, after a full weekend of work around the house, Mom and Dad would sit at the dining-room table with worried looks on their faces, a pile of yellow bills and a black checkbook in front of them. Monday morning early, Dad would pack up his bag, kiss Mom and start back for the sawmill.

Late in August Dad took Mom and me up to the Mountain for a couple of weeks' vacation. Because there was no way to take Pooch that far in our car, Dad asked one of his hunting friends, a man with the name Claggert, to care for Pooch.

Claggert's house was not painted, with an old car, and a bunch of kids running around barefoot. The youngest child was about a year and a half, and there was something wrong with his legs. He lay on the porch in a box while the other kids played around him. I tried not to look at him, yet I couldn't keep from glancing at him.

Claggert tied Pooch to a clothesline so she could run, and Dad and I left. It was hard to listen to her sad crying as we drove away.

Surprisingly, I didn't have a good time on the Mountain. Dad was to too busy to take me swimming or fishing, and later at night, lying under heavy quilts, I could hear my parents whisper about the bad times at the sawmill.

During the day, I'd play on the logs. Imissed my friends, and I even began to wish that Pooch ws with me. At least then I could run up and down the dirt roads with someone, and we could run after squirrels and hunt deer together. At the end of two weeks, I was ready to go home.

When Dad and I drove back to the Claggerts', the whole family came out on the porch. Mrs. Claggert was holding the baby with the disabled legs. Mr. Claggert steppped forward with one of his sons about my age. He had Pooch on a rope.

"Hi, Bill," Claggert said. He seemed cheerful, but his eyes avoided ours. While he talked with my dad, I went over and patted Pooch on the head. Her big tail beat the ground, and she licked my hand. But in an unsusual way she sat politely still, as if they had taught her some manners. The kid gave me a funny look.

"That dog's something," Claggert was saying. "The kids just love her. Smart! Bobbie. here taught Pooch to pull the little one around in a wagon."

"Yes," Dad said, "she'll be a good hunting dog soon as I get around to train her."

Claggert cleared his throat. "You ever think of selling her, Bill?"

"No, I won't sell it."

"I give you $50 for her."

I held my breath. Fifty dollars was a unheard-of amount. Suddenly I was worried that Dad might take it, considering the bad times at the mill. Pooch was Part of our family. You don't sell family.

"No," my dad said, "she's just an ordinary hunting dog."

"A hundred dollars."

Where would this poor Claggert get a hundred dollars? Something was wrong here. I saw a strange expression grow on Dad's face. "I don't want anything for her," he said firmly. "I just want my dog."

"Give her here, Bobbie." Claggert said and signed his son back to the porch. When my father tried to take the rope, Claggert held on.

"I'll fight you for her if I have to, Bill, but I have to keep this dog."

My father regarded Claggert the way I'd seen him examine a snake that he'd kick or walk around. His hands held. "I told you she's not for sale."

"I can't give her to you, Bill," Claggert begged. "My wife and kids won't let me." His face was twisted with pain.

"You know my littlest has got bad legs. My wife puts him out on a blanket in the front yard there, and the kids are supposed to watch him. The other day, though, the kids were playing, and the baby crawled out in the road. My wife looked out the kitchen window and saw the baby lying on the road with a car roaring down on him! She screamed. Then Pooch jumped over the fence, ran up and grabbed the bady by his pants and pulled him out of the road. Sure enough, it was some drunk that stopped the car right over where the baby was."

Claggert cleared his throat. "Pooch saved his life."

Claggert looked at my father with eager eyes. "We love that dog. My wife makes her up a bed in the baby's room every night. We'll take care of her till the day she dies. I'll pay you any amount, Bill."

My father waited in silence, then let go of the rope. "Well, I told you I'm not selling her." He bent down and stroked Pooch behind both ears and ran his hand gently down her smooth face. "So I'll give her to you."

Claggert let out a cry of surprise, took Dad's hand and shook it hard. He looked back at his family on the porch and, with a big smile, nodded.

"Come on." Dad said to me, and he started back down the dirt road to the car.

"Why'd you give her away?" I cried, tears coming to my eyes. "She is yours!" But I was thinking, She's mine! I feed and water her. I take her running.

Dad picked me us in his arms. "listen, son. There isn't a living thing a man can hold on to in this world unless he loves it and works for it. Those folks love Pooch better than me. By rights she belongs to them."

They don't love her better than me, my heart cried, now that it was too late.

"Come on, be big. I know what you're fealing."

He opened the car door and put me inside, up front with him.

"Yes, sir." I said, coughing back tears.

Dad got in, started the engine and then did something he now often did. He put his arm around my shoulder, drew me close and kept me by his side all the way home.

That fall, to support his family, my father finally had to give up his sawmill and take a job he did not like in a paper factory. But I had learned something important from him -- something more important than a lesson in loss. He showed me, in a world ruined by misfortune, what effort and sacrifice and kindness it takes to be a man -- to hold on to the important things and keep safe the ones you love.
