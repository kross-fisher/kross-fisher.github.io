No Footprints in the Snow

Anonymous

Charley was going blind, out of work, and angry, and did not want to be interrupted by even the President -- much less by the stranger at the door.

It was early Tuesday afternoon, December 11, 1951. There was a wet, wind-griven storm, blowing in from the Atlantic Ocean 25 miles away. More than six inches had piled up when darkness closed in at 4:00 p. m.

Charley Hall, 59, a large, strong farmer, hit angry fists on the wheel of his old truck as he drove absent-mindedly through the storm toward home three and a half miles north of Lisbon Falls, Maine.

Almost blind, he drove the shaking vehicle over Bowdoinham Road more from emmory than skill. Dismissed from the mill three months before, he felt terrible.

The food supplies he needed for his wife and five children during the winter were already running out. He'd gone into town to beg for his old job. "I'm sorry, Charley, but we just can't put you back to work, not with your poor eyesight." hsi former boss had said an hour earlier. He turned the wheel hard and turned into the driveway. The back of his truck slid on the ice and crashed into the mailbox. His face twisted in anger as he stepped hard on the gas.

Edie, the 7-year-old daughter, stared through the living room window and watched worriedly as her father approached the house. "Daddy's home!" she shouted. Her tone was a warning, not an announcement. Dropping the curtain over the window, Edie fled to her unheated room in the attic. She turned on the lamp for warmth and crawled with it under thick covers. This was the one place she could escape the violence below.

At 5:30 the family gathered silently, cautiously, in the kitchen for supper. The meal was always the same: corn or tomato with milk, and homemade bread. There was no conversation.

An hour later, Charley turned on an old radio to begin an evening listening to his favorite programs. But this was no pleasant portrait of a poor family spending an evening together. Their family was poor, but what was worse, it was a family that lacked love and normal affection.

They came together, not because of warm human feelings but because the kitchen was the only warm room in the old house. Edie sat at the table, biting her lower lip as she cut paper clothes out of a book to put on her cardboard dolls. Melvin, aged 11, knew she wanted somebody to play with, so he sat down and offer to help.

"Only girls play with dolls." Ben, 13, laughed at him. Melvin's face became angry and red. But before he could reply, his father hit his fist hard on the kitchen table. "Shut up so I can hear my programs!" he roared.

Edie blinked back a tear and looked at the Christmas tree standing near the front window in the living room. She and Melvin had selected the tree, cut it down, and carried it home. Most of the decorations vere hand-made. The only decorations that had been bought were the lights and a few delicate balls their grandmother had given the family. And one more thing: on top of the tree stood a china angel with arms sticking out toward heven.

"Do you believe angels really exist?" Edie asked Melvin in a whisper as she tried to break the silence in the room. Melvin shrugged. "I don't know. But they talk about them in church, so maybe they do."

A loud knocking at the front door interrupted their conversation. A late-night caller. "Charley, somebody at the door," Elsie, his 37-year-old wife said. "Well, go see who it is and get rid of him," he said impatiently, "Just leave me alone with the programs."

A tall man with a beard stood at the door. He smiled pleasantly when Elsie opened the door and stared at him in the darkness. Standing behind her mother, Edie shyly watched the stranger. "I'm sorry to disturb you so late, but I need to speak with Mr. Hall." the man said.

"Well... I don't know..." Elsie said.

"It's very important. Would you tell him I'd like to speak with him?"

She hesitated. "I'll tell him, but I don't think he's... ah... interested in talking with anybody."

While he waited, the man knelt down on one knee by Edie and pointed at the Christmas tree. "I'll bet you made a lot of those lovely decorations," he said. "Yes I did." she admitted, pleased that he'd noticed. She liked the kindness in his eyes and the warmth in this voice. She wondered what it would be like to have a daddy like him.

He looked over the tree slowly and stopped when he saw the angel. She watched him studying the decorations. "Do you believe in angels?" she asked shyly. "Oh yes indeed!" he said firmly. "Don't you?" "I don't know," she admitted honestly. "I wish I did, though, I'd like to meet one someday."

The man gave a quiet laughter. "Maybe you have and just didn't realize it," he suggested. She turned and looked into the smilling eyes. "How would I know?" she asked.

The question was interrupted by the sound of her father hitting his fist on the table and shouting, "I don't care if he's the president himself -- get rid of him!"

"Don't you worry, Edie," the man said as he stood up. "Angels always let you know in someway. It's God's way of showing His love."

Her mother returned, her lips tight. Before she could speak, the stranger reached into his coat pocket and pulled out a white envelope. "I'm sorry to have disturbed your family tonight," he said, handing her the envelope. "I just wanted to leave this with you and tell Mr. Hall to report back to work at the mill tomorrow. He's got a new job there."

He leaned toward the little girl, and kissed her lightly on the cheek. "Merry Christmas. And don't forget what I said, Edie: Angels always let you know. No Footprints."

"What a strange man!" Elsie whispered as she nervously tore the envelope open. The contents slipped through her trembling fingers and fell to the floor; three $100 bills. For a long moment, mother and daughter simply stared at the money and the door where the stranger ahd stood moments before. Edie recovered first, picked the three bills up, and handed them to her mother. They both ran toward the kitchen.

"Charley, you'll never believe what I've got!" Elsie cried.

"I don't care what you've got. Will you please shut--"

"Don't you dare tell me to shut up!" she interrupted. "You look at this!"

The usually nervous woman stood firmly in front of her husband and held the money out for his inspection. Shocked by the strength in her voice, his jaw dropped as he silently stared at her in astonishment. Then he saw the money.

"What's that?" he asked in disbelief, pointing at the bills.

"That is what the man at the door came to give you -- along with some good news. You go back to work at the mill tomorrow," she said as tears filled her eyes. "I told you God would take care of us."

Charley got clumsily out of his chair. "Where'd the man go? Call him back so I can talk to him."

"That's funny; I didn't hear his car leaving." Edie said before her mother could reply.

"Get me the lantern!" Charley said. "He couldn't have walked far in this snow."

Edie had already run ahead and picked the lantern off the hook near the front door. Her father struck a match, lit it, and stepped outside into the darkness.

"Hello!" he called. "Is anybody there?" Only a few flakes danced in the air. "I don't see anything." the nearly blind man compained.

Edie pushed past her father. "Daddy, look at the ground," she whispered. "What is it? What do you see?" he asked anxiously.

"Dad, it stopped snowing before the man came. But there are no footprints or tracks of any kind in the snow."

Silently the family went back inside and closed the door. Elsie and the children looked at one another as Charley stood with his back toward them. He kept clearing his throat and rubbing the back of his hand over his eyes. "I just don't understand it!" he whispered again and again.

Edie pulled on her mother's arm. "Mama, how did that man know my name?" she asked. "I don't know, dear." Suddenly, a broad, delighted smile spread over Edie's face as she looked at the angel on top of the Christmas tree and remembered the stranger's last words to her: Angels always let you know.

Epilogue: This event in 1951 was the beginning of a change for the better in Charley Hall's life. He turned his life around died a happy man 30 years later. I know because Edie, the 7-year-old child in the story, grew up and became my wife.
