My First Love

by James Stewart-Gordon

Watching old movies on late-night TV is sometimes a magic carpet that carries you to an earlier time. Recently, I found myself transported back to the summer when I discovered that being young and in love is tough enough, but being young, in love and too shy to say anything about it can take a long time to forget. Sometimes you never do.

At 15, going on 16, I had never known any girls. Throughout the week I played football or baseball, and boxed every Friday night at the school (all boys) boxing club. Saturdays, if the school didn't have a game, I headed for the movie house where for a little money you could have a head full of dreams. The plots of the romantic films of those days were pretty much the same, but to me they were a mirror of life as it should be -- a lovely girl met a self-confident young man, who courted her with clever language instead of flowers.

One rainy Saturday, after seeing a romantic movie I walked into the grocery store next door to the movie. Behind the candy counter was a girl I had never seen before. She was a blonde, about my age, and when she smiled she had a dimple. She was the most beautiful girl I had ever seen. I wanted to give her my brilliant smile and joke with her, but all I could do was point at a tray and say in a voice that trembled and broke: "Can I have a nickel's worth, please?"

She got the chocolate, weighed it and put it into a white paper bag. Our hands nearly touched when I handed her my money. It was not until I got home that I realized I had been too astonished even to open the bag.

For the next week I lived in a dream world filled with a blonde with a dimple, who smiled as I talked to her in bright dialogue learned from the movies.

I began my double life the following Saturday. Our team had a game scheduled, but I was up early planning for another look at my recently discovered love goddess. At the team bus, I made one of those decisions that have been known to change men's lives. "Coach," I said, "My mother has had a bad fall and I have to get right home. I'll have to miss the game." Before he could question me, I sped off.

The movie did not open for another hour. I walked past the store several times, saw the girl, started to go inside and then stopped -- I desperately wanted to say something to her, but I could think of nothing. Besides, I had begun feeling guilty about telling the coach a lie.

Then I thought of a line from a movie: "Say, you're new around here." the words seemed perfect. There was a park nearby, and I sat on a bench, practicing my opening line.

when I finally got myself inside the store, trying as hard as I could to walk easily, I went over to the candy counter, looking for the blonde. A dark-haired girl with long earrings was there instead. Before I realized it, the line I had been practicing jumped to my lips, "Say," I said, "You're new around here."

I lost my self-confidence completely then and fled to the movie house, where I sat, unable to follow the first five minutes of the movie.

It was dark when I left the movie, my spirits had somewhat returned, and I decided to try the store again. The blonde with the dimple was standing behind the counter. I walked as steadily as I could toward her, tried to smile like the hero in the movie. I had never spent more than a nickel before on candy, but I felt this time I should spend more. Tossing my last coin on the counter, I said, "Make it a dime's worth."

She smiled, filled the bag and handed it over. I took it with what I felt was a casual gesture and started to say, like the one in the movie, "Be seeing you." But at that moment a very fat women with two very fat children pushed me out of the way with her elbow.

The weeks that followed were weeks of wonder. My head was full of romantic ideas, and I began walking to the store almost daily after school, pretending to study the piles of goods in the windows while I watched for her blond head. I wondered how I could meet her away from the store so I could talk to her. I had never been in love before. In the movies the first steps of boy meets girl always seemed so simple, but the way things happened in real life was more complicated.

One Saturday the girl I had been waiting all week to see came from somewhere in the back of the store -- and before I could say anything she said, "Hello."

Time stood still. When I could finally answer, I stuttered, "I'm going to the movie and I'd like some candy."

She smiled, and I noticed that her eyes were very blue. "Chocolate?"

"Yes, five cents' worth of chocolate." I heard myself saying. I couldn't take my eyes off her as she picked up the pieces and dropped them into a bag. I knew there were three pieces for five cents, but the girl dropped in five pieces and took my nickel without saying anything. She was standing very close to me, her face turned up to me. I was about to thank her when the girl with the long earrings called, "Marie," and she turned away.

So that was her name -- Marie! As in a dream I went to the movie, and several hours later I left the theater saying the name "Marie" over and over to myself.

That Friday at boxing my mind was still so much on Marie that I forgot to move away when Tommy Walgren, the club's worst boxer, struck at my nose. Blood ran from my nose. Later, glad at his success, he took me aside and showed me a picture of a girl in a white dress. "Her name is Marjorie," he said. "I met her last week and she gave me her picture."

I was surprised. "You just met her and she gave you her picture?"

"Sure." Tommy said at once. "I'm goint to meet her tomorrow and give her mine."

"My girl's name is Marie," I said. "She looks like movie actress Joan Bennett."

"Wow," said Walgren. "Joan Bennett. You have her picture?"

My face was red. "It's on the dresser in my bedroom."

"Will you bring it to school next week?" Walgren asked.

"Sure." I said, in a casual way.

It wasn't until I was in bed, still enjoying my moment of triumph, that I realized I had better get a picture of someone who looked like Joan Bennett. Then I remembered they had pictures of movie actress at store. If I could find an unknown who looked a little like Joan Bennett I could show it to Tommy. But I did not find a picture at the store. With a heavy heart, I turned to the candy counter. Marie was there, smiling.

"Hello." she said.

For a brief moment I thought wildly of saying, "Can I have your picture?" But I didn't. I just bought a nickel's worth of chocolate. Somehow I had to get up the courage to ask Marie to meet me where we could talk. But how?

In one of the movies that afternoon a man said to a singing girl, "Meet me after the show." She did, and romance started. The line stuck in my mind, and I determined to try it on Marie. She would be pleased and agree. Then I could tell her how much she looked like Joan Bennett and ask for her picture.

When the movie was over I headed to the candy counter. The dark-haired girl with the earring was there, but no sign of Marie. "Is Marie here?" I asked.

"She went home," the dark-haired girl said. Then she looked at me. "You like her, don't you?"

I tried to say something, but the words would not come. With a red face, I turned and ran out of the store.

On Monday I went to school, very unhappy. Tommy was not there. He was ill and would be out for two weeks. This was nice. But the dark-haired girl's question and my reaction weighed heavily on my mind, and I knew then and there that I could never face going back to see Marie.

When Tommy returned and asked to see Marie's picture, I told him we had broken up. "So did Marjorie and I." he said. Neither of us ever mentioned our girls again.

I did see Marie once more, but that was months later. One evening I had been downtown and was taking the subway home. The door opened and Marie got on. She smiled and came by me side.

"Hello," She said, "have you been away?"

I started to say something, but the noise of the train made it difficult and I had to bend close to Marie's ear. I could smell her perfume and it was wonderful. I could not remember a single line from a movie to tell her. We stood there silently until we were at my stop. As the doors opened there was a small piece of quiet.

"Do you still go to the store?" Marie asked.

"No," I replied. "Not anymore."

"I don't work there anymore either." she said.

My heart jumped. If I could find out where she worked, I could see her again. I started to ask, "Where do you work now?"

She was beside me, her face turned up to mine. But then the door began to close and, without knowing what I was doing, I jumped off before I could get her answer.

That was the last time I ever saw Marie.

The other night, when that old movie on television was winding up, I thought of Marie, my goddess of the candy counter. I had been too young and nto experienced to let her know I wished that I could know her better, and as I got up and turned off the TV, I said to myself, "I bet I could have kissed her..."

I saw my wife on her way upstairs, holding a number of magazines. "Hey," I called to her, "What are you doing after the show?" I sounded just like the man in the movie and my face did not turn red at all.
